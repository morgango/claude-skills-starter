# Writing Rules Reference

This document defines the strict writing constraints used by the **high-level-tech-presentation** skill.

These rules ensure that generated slide text is concise, seller-friendly, and compatible with the Google Slides template.

All slide content generated by the skill must follow these rules before the data object is considered valid.

These rules apply to every field defined in:

`references/data-schema.md`

---

# Core Principle

Slides are designed for **Elastic sellers**, not engineers.

The purpose of slide text is to:

• trigger the idea
• support the conversation
• highlight customer value

Slides are **not documentation**.

If a sentence reads like documentation, it should be rewritten.

---

# Length Rules

Each field must contain **10–12 words maximum**.

Shorter is usually better.

Target length:

• 6–10 words

Why this matters:

The template layout breaks when sentences become too long.

Example:

❌ "This platform provides a distributed orchestration framework for containers across hybrid cloud environments."

✅ "Runs containerized applications reliably across clusters and cloud environments."

---

# Sentence Structure

Each field should express **one idea only**.

Avoid combining multiple clauses or explanations.

Example:

❌ "Automates deployments and monitoring while improving reliability and reducing infrastructure complexity."

✅ "Automates application deployments across clusters."

---

# No Label Prefixes

The slide template already displays section labels.

Generated text must **never repeat those labels**.

Do not start a sentence with phrases like:

• "Works best when"
• "Not a good fit when"
• "Pursue when"
• "Pause when"
• "Next step"

Examples:

❌ "Works best when teams operate large microservice architectures."

✅ "Large microservice environments with frequent deployments."

❌ "Next step: schedule a discovery session."

✅ "Schedule a discovery session with the platform engineering team."

---

# No Em Dashes

Em dashes break formatting in the template.

Never use:

—

Instead use:

• commas
• colons
• semicolons
• sentence restructuring

Examples:

❌ "Built-in orchestration — simplifies operations across environments."

✅ "Built-in orchestration simplifies operations across environments."

---

# Seller-Friendly Language

Write in language a **non-technical Account Executive** could comfortably repeat.

Avoid:

• internal engineering terminology
• implementation details
• architecture descriptions

Examples to avoid:

❌ "Implements distributed control plane reconciliation loops."

❌ "Provides event-driven infrastructure abstraction layers."

Better examples:

✅ "Automates application deployment across clusters."

✅ "Simplifies managing services across environments."

---

# Business Outcomes First

Lead with **customer impact**, not technical features.

Focus on:

• efficiency
• reliability
• cost
• speed
• operational simplicity

Example:

❌ "Uses container orchestration to manage workloads."

✅ "Reduces deployment errors and speeds up application releases."

---

# Required Sentence Structures

Some fields must follow **exact sentence patterns**.

These patterns ensure consistent slide design.


## CUSTOMER_1

Must follow exactly:

"Lets teams [action] to [outcome]."

Example:

"Lets teams deploy applications faster to reduce release delays."


## POSITIONING_LENS

Must follow exactly:

"Evaluate with [criterion1], [criterion2], and [criterion3]."


## CUSTOMER_LENS

Must follow exactly:

"Evaluate with [criterion1], [criterion2], and [criterion3]."


## SELL_LENS

Must follow exactly:

"Qualify in on [criterion1], [criterion2], and [criterion3]."


## NOT_SELL_LENS

Must follow exactly:

"Qualify out on [criterion1], [criterion2], and [criterion3]."


## AE_LENS

Must follow exactly:

"Think about [account type 1], [type 2], and [type 3]."


These sentence structures must match exactly.

Changing them can break slide formatting.

---

# Metrics Rules

Metrics must be credible and defensible.

Avoid exaggerated claims.

Prefer:

• industry benchmarks
• public studies
• realistic estimates

Metric fields follow this structure:

METRIC_NUM

Short value only.

Examples:

3x
40%
1

Do not include units here.


METRIC_LABEL

Short description of the metric.

Examples:

Faster
Less Cost
Unified Platform


METRIC_DESC

One sentence explaining why the metric matters.

Example:

"Faster incident detection reduces downtime and improves service reliability."

---

# Quotation Rules

Some fields must contain **quoted text**.

Required fields:

• LISTEN
• SAY
• ASK

These simulate conversation.

Examples:

LISTEN

"Our deployments keep failing across environments."

SAY

"Elastic helps teams simplify operations by consolidating observability and search."

ASK

"How are you managing deployments across multiple environments today?"

---

# Tone Rules

The tone should feel like **a sales leader explaining something to the field team**.

Tone should be:

• clear
• confident
• practical
• outcome focused

Avoid:

• marketing buzzwords
• technical deep dives
• vague product claims

---

# Final Validation Checklist

Before returning the final data object, verify:

1. Every field is under 12 words.
2. No em dashes appear anywhere.
3. No label prefixes are used.
4. Lens sentences follow the exact required phrasing.
5. Metrics follow the number/label/description structure.
6. Quotes appear where required.

If any rule fails, regenerate the field before producing the final object.
